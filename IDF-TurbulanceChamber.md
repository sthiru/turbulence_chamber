4. **BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF THE INVENTION (250 \- 1000 WORDS)**

Atmospheric turbulence arises from random spatial and temporal fluctuations in the refractive index of air caused by variations in temperature, wind, pressure, and humidity. These refractive index inhomogeneities degrade optical beam propagation. The strength of atmospheric turbulence is commonly quantified by the refractive index structure parameter (Cn2) which serves as a key metric for characterizing optical propagation through the atmosphere. To investigate these effects under controlled laboratory conditions, a turbulence chamber capable of reproducing realistic atmospheric turbulence in a repeatable environment is required.

The present invention relates to a fully automated atmospheric turbulence generation and characterization system capable of generating controlled, repeatable, and user-configurable turbulence conditions for laboratory evaluation of laser beam propagation. Unlike conventional turbulence chambers that rely on manual adjustment of heaters and airflow to achieve desired turbulence conditions, the proposed invention integrates automated calibration, lookup-table-based control, real-time sensing, and closed-loop regulation into a single intelligent platform capable of generating turbulence on demand.

The invention comprises a thermally insulated atmospheric turbulence chamber designed to generate and characterize controlled optical turbulence under laboratory conditions. The chamber incorporates electrically controlled heating elements, independently controlled brushless DC fans, a distributed sensor network, and CMOS camera. Controlled thermal gradients produced across the cross-section of the chamber  by the heating elements, together with regulated airflow generated by the fans, create refractive index fluctuations, with desirable inner and outer scale that emulate atmospheric turbulence over a defined optical path. A network of temperature, pressure, humidity, and airflow sensors continuously monitors the chamber environment, while CMOS camera captures the propagated laser beam to quantify turbulence-induced optical effects. The acquired thermal and optical measurements are processed in real time to estimate and validate the refractive index structure thereby enabling continuous characterization of the generated turbulence.

A distinguishing feature of the invention is a two-stage automated calibration methodology in which the first stage establishes the relationship between fan pulse-width modulation (PWM) commands and the corresponding airflow generated by each fan, while the second stage systematically characterizes the combined influence of heating and airflow on the resulting chamber temperature distribution and turbulence strength. The calibration data are organized into a multidimensional lookup table that maps actuator operating parameters to the corresponding turbulence level.

The multidimensional lookup table enables on-demand generation of user-defined atmospheric turbulence through interpolation, allowing the system to automatically determine the optimum heating and airflow conditions required to achieve the desired refractive index structure parameter (Cn2) without repeated manual calibration. The chamber operates using a closed-loop feedback architecture in which embedded proportional–integral–derivative (PID) controllers continuously regulate the heating elements and airflow to maintain stable and repeatable turbulence conditions while compensating for thermal drift and environmental variations. A web-based Human–Machine Interface (HMI) provides centralized monitoring and control of the system, enabling users to configure operating parameters, initiate automated calibration, monitor real-time sensor measurements, visualize chamber conditions, control actuator settings, and perform synchronized data acquisition. The system further incorporates programmable temperature safety limits that automatically disable the heating elements when predefined operating thresholds are exceeded, ensuring safe and reliable operation.

By integrating automated calibration, multidimensional lookup-table-based interpolation, distributed sensing, optical diagnostics, PID-based closed-loop regulation, and real-time supervisory control into a single platform, the proposed invention provides a compact, intelligent, and programmable atmospheric turbulence chamber capable of generating controlled turbulence on demand. The invention eliminates manual calibration, enhances repeatability, and provides a versatile laboratory platform for atmospheric optics research under controlled environment.

   Several techniques have been developed to emulate atmospheric turbulence under controlled laboratory conditions for evaluating beam propagation. The most used technologies include atmospheric Pseudo Random Phase Plates \[1\], Spatial Light Modulators (SLMs) \[2\], and atmospheric turbulence chambers \[3\] \[4\]. Each of these approaches reproduces certain characteristics of atmospheric turbulence; however, they differ significantly in their operating principles, flexibility, realism, and controllability, and each carries limitations that restrict its ability to reproduce realistic, user-defined, and repeatable turbulence conditions.

   Phase Plates are among the most widely adopted methods for laboratory simulation of optical turbulence. These devices consist of transparent optical plates fabricated with permanently etched or deposited random phase distributions that imitate atmospheric wavefront distortions \[1\]. As a laser beam propagates through the phase plate, the imposed phase variations reproduce optical effects such as wavefront distortion, beam spreading, and scintillation. To emulate temporal turbulence, the phase plate is generally rotated or translated mechanically so that different portions of the random phase distribution interact with the propagating beam. Phase plates provide a simple, compact, and repeatable solution for laboratory testing and are widely employed for validating adaptive optics systems and beam propagation experiments. However, because the random phase distribution is permanently fabricated into the optical element, each phase plate corresponds to a fixed turbulence strength and statistical distribution, and the turbulence characteristics are determined at fabrication and remain fixed for the lifetime of the plate. Continuous adjustment of the refractive index structure parameter is therefore not possible, and simulating temporal turbulence through mechanical rotation or translation produces repetitive, cyclic phase distortions that do not accurately represent the continuously evolving random nature of atmospheric turbulence.

   Another widely adopted approach utilizes Spatial Light Modulators (SLMs). These electronically programmable optical devices generate turbulence by displaying computer-generated random phase screens based on theoretical atmospheric models. The displayed phase patterns can be updated electronically to emulate different turbulence strengths and propagation conditions \[2\], and SLM-based systems offer considerable flexibility because turbulence parameters can be modified through software without changing any hardware components. Such systems are extensively used in experiments like beam propagation studies. Nevertheless, SLMs simulate only optical phase distortions based on mathematical turbulence models rather than generating actual atmospheric turbulence, and their performance is constrained by finite pixel resolution, refresh rate, and diffraction efficiency. Consequently, the turbulence they generate remains a numerical representation of atmospheric effects rather than a physical turbulent medium. A further limitation is that an SLM imposes its phase perturbation at a single discrete plane, whereas real atmospheric turbulence is distributed continuously and randomly in three dimensions along the entire propagation path. This lack of a volumetric interaction means an SLM cannot reproduce the path-integrated, multi-scale amplitude and intensity effects that arise when a beam propagates through a genuinely extended turbulent medium, limiting its realism relative to a physical turbulent volume. 

   Physical atmospheric turbulence chambers constitute another important class of laboratory turbulence generators and provide a more realistic representation of atmospheric turbulence by physically generating temperature gradients and airflow. These chambers recreate atmospheric turbulence by generating controlled thermal gradients and airflow within an enclosed environment, typically using heaters, fans, humidity generators, temperature sensors, airflow sensors, and optical access windows that allow a laser beam to propagate through the turbulent region \[3\] \[4\]. The interaction between heated air, forced convection, and natural buoyancy produces refractive index fluctuations like those encountered in the atmosphere, and several research groups have developed turbulence chambers to study effects like beam wander, scintillation, and wavefront distortion under controlled environmental conditions. However, most reported chambers primarily function as experimental test facilities for studying beam propagation rather than as intelligent turbulence generation systems. Users typically must manually adjust heater temperatures, airflow conditions, humidity levels, and other environmental parameters through repeated trial-and-error until the desired turbulence condition is achieved, which increases experimental setup time, requires significant operator expertise, and reduces repeatability between experiments. Furthermore, conventional turbulence chambers generally establish operating conditions empirically and do not provide an automated calibration framework that directly relates actuator settings to the generated refractive index structure parameter. Most existing systems measure environmental variables or optical effects only after turbulence has been generated, rather than automatically determining the operating parameters necessary to achieve a specified turbulence strength, so producing a user-defined Cn2 often requires repeated experimentation and iterative parameter adjustment.

   The present invention addresses the need for a fully automated, accurate, and repeatable atmospheric turbulence generation system capable of producing user-defined turbulence conditions under controlled laboratory environments. Conventional laboratory techniques either simulate optical phase distortions or require significant manual intervention to generate physical turbulence, making it difficult to consistently reproduce a desired turbulence strength. The proposed invention overcomes these limitations by providing an intelligent atmospheric turbulence chamber that automates the entire process of turbulence generation, characterization, monitoring, and control.

   This invention eliminates this time-consuming trial-and-error process through a systematic two-stage calibration methodology that establishes a direct relationship between actuator operating parameters and the generated turbulence strength. The calibration data are organized into a multidimensional lookup table, enabling interpolation-based determination of the optimum hot plate temperatures and fan operating conditions required to generate a user-specified Cn2. Consequently, the desired turbulence strength can be generated on demand without repeated manual calibration.

   The invention further addresses the lack of intelligent control in existing turbulence generation systems by incorporating a closed-loop feedback architecture that continuously acquires thermal, pressure, humidity, airflow, and optical measurements to estimate and validate the generated turbulence in real time. Embedded PID controllers automatically regulate the heating elements and airflow, compensating for thermal drift and environmental variations to maintain stable and repeatable turbulence conditions throughout the experiment. The integration of a distributed sensor network, optical diagnostics, automated data acquisition, and a web-based Human–Machine Interface (HMI) enables centralized monitoring, control, visualization, and safe operation of the chamber with minimal operator intervention.

   By integrating automated calibration, calibration-based lookup-table mapping, interpolation-driven operating parameter selection, real-time environmental sensing, optical characterization, PID-based closed-loop regulation, and intelligent supervisory control within a single platform, the invention significantly improves the accuracy, repeatability, efficiency, and reliability of laboratory atmospheric turbulence generation. The system provides a versatile experimental platform for laser beam propagation studies, adaptive optics, and other atmospheric optics applications under controlled and user-configurable turbulence conditions.

7. **NOVEL FEATURES OF THE INVENTION:** Please identity the crucial features and provide the explanation why they contribute to an effective solution to the problem. Please provide us with **detailed explanation on the new elements** of your invention which may be a product / process.

The invention's core innovation is turning a physical atmospheric turbulence chamber into an instrument that can be commanded to a user-specified turbulence strength (Cn2**)** on demand, automatically and repeatably, rather than one whose turbulence level is simply an uncontrolled by-product of whatever heater or fan setting happens to be applied. Reported chambers generate real turbulence only through manual or open-loop actuation with no model tying actuator settings to Cn2. The elements below are the crucial new elements that close this gap.

**1\. Two-stage actuator-to-turbulence calibration**

A fan-to-windflow characterization first establishes, for each fan, a repeatable relationship between its drive command and the actual airflow it produces, letting the controller request a physical flow rate rather than a raw duty cycle. This is combined with a systematic hot-plate and fan-speed sweep that records the steady-state turbulence strength produced at each combination of settings. Together these build, for the first time in this class of instrument, a quantitative model relating actuator commands directly to resulting turbulence strength — making the chamber programmable in physical Cn2 units rather than raw temperature or airflow units.

**2\. Lookup-table interpolation for arbitrary, on-demand** Cn2

The calibration results are organized into a multidimensional lookup table indexed by hot-plate temperature and fan speed, with interpolation used to compute the operating point for any Cn2value the user requests, not merely the discrete combinations that were originally tested. This converts a one-time calibration effort into continuous, arbitrary Cn2 selection across the calibrated envelope, eliminating the repeated manual trial-and-error that fixed test-case or single-ramp chambers require to reach a specific turbulence condition.

**3\. Closed-loop control of turbulence strength, not just temperature**

Embedded PID controllers regulate the hot plates and fans against the calibrated Cn2 setpoint continuously during operation, compensating in real time for thermal drift, ambient variation, and other disturbances so the requested turbulence level is held stable for the full duration of an experiment. The feedback variable driving this loop is the calibrated relationship to Cn2, not temperature alone — turning the apparatus from a temperature-regulated heater into a turbulence-regulated instrument.

**4\. Real-time dual-channel** Cn2 **validation used as a live control signal**

Independent thermal and optical Cn2 estimates are computed continuously and cross-checked against each other and the calibration prediction while an experiment runs. The thermal channel derives the temperature structure constant from spatially separated sensor pairs and converts it to Cn2 via the Tatarski relation; the optical channel computes beam-centroid variance from camera images and converts it to Cn2 through the beam-wander relation, independently yielding the Fried parameter, phase variance, and Rytov variance. Rather than being a post-experiment comparison of models, this dual estimate is an operating sensor input that confirms, moment to moment, that the commanded turbulence is being produced.

**5\. Integrated on-demand control software and HMI**

A distributed software stack — firmware-level PID regulation and sensor streaming, a host server exposing REST endpoints and WebSocket streams, and a browser-based HMI for setting a target Cn2, viewing live temperature and video data, and triggering calibration or capture runs — operationalizes the calibration and closed-loop elements above into a system an operator can invoke directly. A hard safety envelope (bounded hot-plate setpoints with automatic shutoff on over-temperature) is enforced throughout, allowing largely unattended, remotely monitored operation.

Existing physical chambers generate real turbulence but leave the operator to discover, case by case, the actuator settings that produce a given turbulence level, or to accept whatever level a fixed heating profile happens to yield. By combining calibration-derived actuator/Cn2 mapping, interpolation, closed-loop regulation, dual real-time validation, purpose-built sensing, and an integrated control interface, this invention delivers accurate, repeatable, user-defined turbulence generation.

   ## **Overview and Principle of Operation**

The present invention is an intelligent, closed-loop atmospheric turbulence chamber system that generates controlled, physically realistic optical turbulence in a laboratory environment and automatically maps a user-specified turbulence strength, expressed as the refractive index structure parameter Cn2, onto the actuator conditions required to produce it. Rather than requiring an operator to manually adjust heaters and fans and observe whatever turbulence level results, the invention combines a purpose-built thermally isolated chamber, a distributed network of thermal, atmospheric, flow, and optical sensors, a two-stage automated calibration procedure, a multidimensional lookup table with interpolation, embedded closed-loop proportional-integral-derivative (PID) control, and a networked software and human-machine interface (HMI) architecture into a single integrated instrument. The invention may be embodied both as an apparatus, the chamber and its associated hardware and control electronics, and as a method, the calibration, interpolation, and closed-loop control procedure by which a specified turbulence strength is reached and maintained; the two operate together as a single system.

In operation, the chamber is first calibrated, once, to establish a quantitative relationship between actuator settings and the resulting turbulence strength. Thereafter, an operator specifies a desired Cn2 through a web-based interface. The system automatically interpolates the hot-plate temperature and fan speed expected to produce that value from the calibration data, commands the actuators accordingly, and engages embedded PID controllers that hold the chamber at the calculated operating point despite thermal drift or ambient disturbance. While turbulence is being generated, a distributed sensor network and an exit-plane camera continuously measure the actual chamber and beam conditions, from which the system computes two independent, cross-validating estimates of Cn2  in real time, confirming that the requested turbulence is in fact being produced and logging the result. A desired turbulence condition can therefore be requested once, generated automatically, held stable for the duration of an experiment, and continuously validated, without the repeated manual trial-and-error that characterizes conventional laboratory turbulence generators. The invention is applicable to laser beam propagation studies requiring a controllable, repeatable, and quantitatively characterized turbulent medium.

## **System Architecture and Component Functions**

1. ### **Chamber Enclosure**

   The chamber enclosure forms the physical core of the invention. The size of the chamber is 2 x 0.6 x 0.3 m3   (lxbxw) and it is thermally insulated and designed to minimize the influence of external environmental perturbations, so that the turbulence generated inside is attributable to controlled internal actuation rather than uncontrolled laboratory conditions. Optical access windows at the entrance and exit apertures allow a collimated laser beam to enter, propagate through the active turbulent region along a central optical axis, and exit for downstream measurement, while the internal geometry provides a representative interaction length that is sufficient for the beam to accumulate a measurable, physically meaningful distortion while remaining compact enough to house the distributed sensors and actuators described below. Its function is to provide a stable, isolated, and optically accessible volume within which turbulence can be generated and measured repeatably.

2. ### **Heating and Airflow Actuation Subsystems**

The heating subsystem consists of two electrically controlled hot plates, each switched through a solid-state relay, whose function is to establish the thermal gradients from which optical turbulence arises. The airflow subsystem consists of four 24-volt DC brushless fans, each driven through a MOSFET switching stage under pulse-width modulation, whose function is to establish and control the convective mixing of air within the chamber. Acting together, the interaction of the heat introduced by the hot plates with the forced convection produced by the fans generates spatially and temporally varying temperature gradients, which in turn produce the refractive-index fluctuations that constitute optical turbulence along the beam path; varying temperature and fan speed independently allows turbulence strengths ranging from weak to strong to be produced purely through electrical control.

3. ### **Microcontroller and Safety Control**

A microcontroller unit governs both actuator subsystems, performing low-level PID regulation of the hot plates and fans and enforcing a safety-critical operating envelope: hot-plate setpoints are constrained to 0–120°C, and the hot plates are automatically disabled if the measured temperature exceeds a predefined threshold, independent of any setpoint requested by the higher-level software. A status LED indicates the controller's operating state. Its function is to provide fast, safety-enforced, low-level actuation that remains reliable even if the networked software layers above it fault or disconnect.

4. ### **Distributed Sensor Network**

A heterogeneous sensor network provides the situational awareness needed for both control and characterization. Twelve digital temperature sensors are connected to a shared single-wire digital bus, fitted with a 4.7 kΩ pull-up resistor, and sampled at a default interval of 1 second, providing distributed temperature measurements throughout the chamber volume. These sensors are arranged in a specific geometry: ten of the twelve are positioned in two longitudinal columns running the length of the chamber, forming pairs at each of the four fan locations plus one additional pair near the optical beam path, with the two sensors in each pair separated along the airflow axis at fixed distances of 300 mm and 500 mm from the fan plane; the remaining two sensors are placed immediately upstream and immediately downstream of the turbulent region along the beam path itself. Two combined pressure/temperature sensors, interfaced over SPI, measure barometric pressure and ambient temperature, with one sensor mounted inside the chamber to monitor internal conditions and a second mounted outside the chamber to record the ambient laboratory environment for comparison; the internal pressure and temperature values are also used directly in the thermal estimate of Cn2. Two humidity sensors likewise provide internal and external relative humidity readings. Four analog airflow sensors, one associated with each fan, are positioned a fixed 40 cm downstream of their respective fan to obtain a representative and repeatable measurement of the generated airflow while avoiding the immediate fan-blade wake; the raw analog output of each airflow sensor is converted into a physical windflow value through the polynomial/linear calibration described below. A Gigabit Ethernet camera, controlled through a software development kit, is positioned at the exit plane of the chamber and captures grayscale images of the beam spot after it has propagated through the turbulent region; the camera is configured by default to capture monochrome 8-bit images with a 10,000 μs exposure and unity gain, though these settings can be overridden through a user-supplied configuration file. The function of this network, collectively, is to supply the raw physical measurements from which turbulence strength is both controlled and independently verified. All sensor data generated by the microcontroller are transmitted over a dedicated serial connection operating at 250,000 baud to the single-board host computer for further processing.

5. ### **Host Processing and Software Architecture**

A single-board host computer and its software stack form the processing and control layer. During data capture sessions, the host server polls the microcontroller at a configurable interval, 1 second by default, and simultaneously triggers synchronized camera image capture; the resulting records are saved to a CSV file, with each entry containing a timestamp, all sensor values, and the calculated Cn2, while the corresponding camera images are stored in a timestamped directory with references back to the associated data record. The control system itself is implemented as a distributed software stack spanning three levels: embedded firmware performs real-time PID regulation of the hot plates and streams sensor data upward over the serial link; a Python-based host server provides a web interface over TCP/IP, exposing REST endpoints for sensor queries and actuator commands and managing WebSocket streams for real-time monitoring and video, with a background polling loop that collects microcontroller status, computes the thermal Cn2 estimate, triggers camera capture during active sessions, and broadcasts updates to connected clients; and a browser-based HMI, served as static web files, renders live temperature charts and provides interactive controls for fan speed, hot-plate setpoints, calibration initiation, and data capture. Its function is to convert raw sensor and calibration data into actionable control decisions and to give the operator centralized visibility and command from a standard web browser. The software architecture enables fully automated calibration runs, data logging, and real-time visualization, and additionally provides a fallback simulation mode for off-hardware testing of the optical processing pipeline, allowing the software to be developed and validated independently of the physical apparatus.

6. ### **Calibration Subsystem**

The two-stage calibration procedure is itself a functional component of the invention, converting raw actuator commands into a quantitative, invertible turbulence model. The first stage, fan-to-windflow sensor characterization, establishes a linear relationship between the fan PWM command, S, in the range 0 to 255, and the windflow sensor output, Q, assumed to take the form

                                                       *Q \= aS \+ b*                                                                  (1)           

Each fan–sensor pair is calibrated independently: the fan speed is stepped from 0 to 255 in increments of 5 PWM units, giving 52 setpoints per fan; at each setpoint the system waits 2 s for the flow to stabilize and then records five sensor readings at 0.2 s intervals, retaining their mean as the calibrated value for that operating point. The slope a and intercept b are obtained by ordinary least-squares regression, with the quality of fit quantified by the coefficient of determination, R²; calibration runs with R² \> 0.9 are accepted for control purposes. The inverse mapping is used for closed-loop control,

                                                      *S \= (Qtarget − b) / a*                                                       (2)

with the computed command clamped to the valid range \[0, 255\]. Ambient temperature, pressure, and humidity are recorded at the start of each calibration session to enable comparison of results across different environmental conditions. The second stage, hot-plate 4-D calibration, constructs a four-dimensional lookup table mapping hot-plate temperature and fan speed to the resulting chamber temperature gradient and Cn2, following a nested protocol in which, for each of four fan speeds, 255, 191, 128, and 64 PWM, the hot-plate temperature is stepped from 80°C to 120°C in 2°C increments (21 temperature steps); at each combination both hot plates are set to the identical target temperature, the system waits 60 s for initial thermalization, and all sensor temperatures are then recorded for 900 s at 10 s intervals. This procedure produces 84 operating points in total, 21 temperature steps multiplied by 4 fan speeds, and for each operating point the steady-state values are estimated from the final 30% of the 15-minute recording window to minimize transient effects. The resulting matrices are stored with hot-plate temperatures as rows and fan speeds as columns, enabling efficient bilinear interpolation for intermediate setpoints, so that the operating conditions required for any requested Cn2, not only the 84 directly calibrated points, can be determined automatically.

## **Operational Interaction Among Components**

The components interact as a continuous cycle that begins with calibration and ends with validated, sustained turbulence generation. During calibration, the host software commands the fan and hot-plate actuators through the characterization sweeps described above, stepping fan PWM in 5-unit increments across 52 setpoints per fan and hot-plate temperature in 2°C increments across 21 steps at each of 4 fan speeds, while the sensor network and camera record the chamber's response over the prescribed 2 s, 60 s, and 900 s stabilization and recording windows; the host server processes this response into the fan PWM-to-windflow model of equations (1) and (2) and the 84-point, temperature-by-fan-speed lookup table. During normal operation, a user-requested Cn2 is translated by the lookup table and bilinear interpolation logic into hot-plate and fan setpoints, which are transmitted to the microcontroller; the microcontroller's embedded PID loops drive the hot plates and fans toward and hold them at that operating point, within the enforced 0–120°C safety envelope, while independently disabling the hot plates on any over-temperature fault. As the chamber responds, the twelve temperature sensors, the internal and external pressure/temperature and humidity sensors, the four airflow sensors, and the exit-plane camera stream live data at 1 s polling intervals back over the 250,000-baud serial link to the host server, which computes the thermal and optical  Cn2 estimates of equations (3) and (4) in real time, derives the Fried parameter, phase variance, and Rytov variance of equations (5) through (7), logs synchronized CSV data records and timestamped camera images, and pushes live updates to the web-based HMI. The operator, through the HMI, observes the requested and achieved turbulence conditions converge and remain stable, and can adjust the target Cn2 at any time, restarting the same closed-loop cycle at a new operating point. Each component therefore has a distinct role, the hot plates and fans generate the physical turbulence, the microcontroller provides low-level actuation and safety, the sensors and camera provide raw and validating measurement, the host server provides calibration, computation, and coordination, and the HMI provides visibility and command, but none of these functions alone produces the invention's capability; it is their tight, closed-loop integration around the calibration-derived turbulence model that allows a user-specified turbulence strength to be reached and held automatically.

## **Novelty and Inventive Features**

The novel aspects of the invention lie in this integration and in the specific mechanisms that enable it. Conventional turbulence chambers generate real turbulence but leave the actuator-to-turbulence relationship uncharacterized, forcing an operator into manual trial-and-error to reach a desired condition, while purely numerical simulators can target an exact turbulence strength precisely only by abandoning a physical turbulent medium altogether. This invention is novel, first, in combining a calibration-derived model relating actuator commands to turbulence strength, obtained from a defined, repeatable 52-point-per-fan and 84-point chamber sweep and expressed quantitatively through equations (1) and (2), with bilinear interpolation that extends that model to continuous, on-demand turbulence selection rather than a fixed set of pre-tested conditions. It is novel, second, in closing the control loop around the turbulence strength itself, through PID regulation driven by the calibrated relationship and bounded by an independently enforced 0–120°C safety envelope, rather than around a simple temperature setpoint, so that the apparatus is a turbulence-regulated instrument rather than a temperature-regulated heater. It is novel, third, in cross-validating the achieved turbulence in real time using two independent, physically distinct measurement channels, a distributed twelve-sensor thermal-gradient channel and a camera-based beam-centroid-variance channel, equations (3) and (4), as a live control and confirmation signal rather than as a single estimate or an offline post-experiment comparison. It is novel, fourth, in deriving a full suite of standard turbulence-characterization metrics, equations (5) through (7), automatically and continuously alongside Cn2 itself, rather than as a separate analysis step. The advantages of the present invention follow directly from the way it generates and controls turbulence, which is fundamentally different from the way each existing laboratory technique does so. Rather than treating differentiation and advantage as separate topics, they are best understood as cause and effect: the invention's departures from phase plates, spatial light modulators, and conventional physical chambers, at the level of physical mechanism and control architecture, are precisely what produce its measured accuracy, repeatability, and operational benefits. Each of the three established techniques solves only part of the underlying problem, physical realism without on-demand control, electronic flexibility without a physical medium, or physical turbulence without a characterized actuator response, and it is by resolving all three limitations simultaneously that the present invention produces results that none of the existing approaches, taken individually, can achieve.

## **A Physically Real, Electronically Governed Turbulent Medium**

Atmospheric phase plates reproduce turbulence by etching or depositing a permanent, fixed random phase pattern onto an optical element; the turbulence strength and statistical character a plate presents are therefore fixed at the time of manufacture. Any change in operating condition requires either substituting a different plate or mechanically rotating or translating the existing one so that a different region of the same fixed pattern intercepts the beam, which produces a repetitive, cyclic distortion rather than a genuinely evolving one. The present invention shares none of this rigidity: it contains no fixed optical pattern at all. Turbulence strength is set purely electrically, through hot-plate temperature and fan speed, and is continuously regulated in closed loop, so the same physical apparatus can be commanded to any calibrated condition on demand, and because the turbulent medium is real, convecting air rather than a static optical element, the resulting fluctuations evolve continuously and randomly in time, in the same manner as the atmosphere itself, rather than cycling mechanically.

Spatial light modulators depart from phase plates by allowing the phase pattern to be reprogrammed electronically, offering considerable flexibility without moving parts. Yet the turbulence they present remains entirely synthetic: it is a numerical phase screen, computed from a mathematical model, rather than a physical medium the beam actually propagates through, and its fidelity is further bounded by the modulator's pixel resolution, refresh rate, and diffraction efficiency. The present invention differs at exactly this point: it generates turbulence through an actual physical process, heated and mechanically mixed air producing genuine, three-dimensional, time-varying refractive-index fluctuations, so that a beam traversing the chamber experiences the same underlying physical mechanism responsible for turbulence outdoors, including amplitude and intensity effects such as scintillation and beam spreading that a phase-only numerical model cannot reproduce. 

Conventional physical turbulence chambers already reported in the literature are the invention's closest relatives, since they too generate turbulence by physically heating and moving air. The difference lies entirely in how that turbulence is arrived at and governed. Existing chambers are operated by manually setting heater power, fan speed, and other environmental parameters and then measuring whatever turbulence level results, or by running a single fixed heating ramp and accepting whatever range of strengths that ramp happens to sweep through over time; in either case, reaching a specific, previously untested Cn2 requires a fresh, manual, trial-and-error experiment, and the actuator-to-turbulence relationship itself is never captured in a form the system can reuse. The present invention instead characterizes that relationship once, systematically, across a calibrated grid of hot-plate temperature and fan-speed combinations, and applies interpolation over the result to compute automatically the settings needed for any requested turbulence strength within range. A closed feedback loop, built on embedded PID control, is then closed directly around that calibrated relationship, and the outcome is continuously confirmed using two independent measurement channels, one thermal and one optical, operating in real time. Existing chambers are, in this sense, open-loop sources whose output must be measured after the fact; the present invention is a closed-loop instrument whose output is specified in advance and then automatically achieved, sustained, and verified.

## **What These Differences Produce in Practice**

Because actuator settings are derived from systematic calibration rather than operator estimation, and because the achieved condition is checked continuously against two independent measurements rather than assumed, the invention reaches a requested turbulence strength with markedly greater accuracy than manual operation allows, and any deviation from the target is visible immediately rather than discovered only during post-experiment analysis. This same calibration-driven basis makes the result repeatable: since the model and the control logic built on it are deterministic, the same requested condition produces the same commanded settings and the same regulated response every time, removing the operator-to-operator and day-to-day variability inherent in manually adjusting heaters and fans by feel. In direct experimental terms, this precision has been confirmed by the close agreement obtained between the thermal and optical Cn2 estimates, which, for a representative 14°C sensor temperature difference, track each other to the same order of magnitude, and by the ability of the closed loop to hold a requested turbulence condition within a narrow band for run times of twenty minutes or longer rather than allowing it to drift.

Because the actuator-to-turbulence relationship is captured once and reused through interpolation rather than rediscovered by trial and error each time, reaching a desired condition is also far faster: what would otherwise be a process of iterative manual adjustment collapses into a single automated convergence, reducing both experimental setup time and the level of specialist expertise an operator needs. This same automation is what makes it practical to schedule a specific, quantitatively known turbulence condition in advance, rather than accept whatever a given heating profile happens to produce, and it does so without trading away safety, since the temperature envelope and automatic shutdown are enforced independently at the firmware level regardless of what the higher-level calibration and control software is doing.

Because turbulence is generated by a real physical medium rather than a numerical approximation, the invention also reproduces effects, such as intensity scintillation and beam spreading, that a phase-only device cannot, and because the chamber's compact interaction length, of the order of one metre, can be tuned to reproduce the cumulative effect of several hundred metres of real outdoor propagation, kilometre-scale atmospheric phenomena become accessible on a laboratory bench without an outdoor test range, without exposure to uncontrolled weather, and without the cost and poor repeatability of field testing. 

Finally, because the closed loop continuously compensates for thermal drift and ambient disturbance rather than leaving the chamber to an open-loop heating profile, the generated turbulence remains stable for the full duration of an experiment, which is what makes extended measurements. And because the safety envelope and a software-based simulation fallback mode are both decoupled from the correctness of the networked calibration and control logic, the resulting instrument is not only more accurate and repeatable than existing chambers, phase plates, and spatial light modulators individually, but also more robust: it can be operated safely even under a software fault, and its control and analysis pipeline can be developed and validated even without the physical chamber present.

Taken together, these effects are not incidental improvements layered onto an otherwise conventional chamber; they are the direct, traceable consequence of replacing a manually operated or purely numerical turbulence source with a calibrated, closed-loop, dually-validated one. Where a phase plate offers repeatability without adjustability, and an SLM offers adjustability without physical realism, and a conventional chamber offers physical realism without a characterized, on-demand response, the present invention offers all three together, and it is this combination, rather than any single feature considered on its own, that constitutes both its technical distinction from prior approaches and the practical advantage it delivers to a laboratory user who needs a specific, known, and stable turbulence condition without extensive manual effort.

12. **UTILITY OF THE INVENTION** (indicate possible application area(s) of the invention)

The invention has utility as a laboratory instrument for producing and studying atmospheric optical turbulence under controlled, repeatable, and quantitatively known conditions, in place of uncontrolled outdoor testing. Its principal application is free-space laser beam propagation research, where it generates turbulence-induced effects, beam wander, scintillation, beam spreading, and wavefront distortion, at a known, user-specified strength, supporting the study and validation of these effects and of theoretical and numerical propagation models against real, physically generated turbulence rather than simulated data alone.

More broadly, it serves free-space optical communication research and general atmospheric-optics research and instrumentation, offering a compact, safely automated, and repeatable turbulence source in place of outdoor test ranges, and functioning as a reference instrument for benchmarking other turbulence-emulation techniques under identical, reproducible conditions.
